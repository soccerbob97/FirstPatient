What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do.

We are building an AI-native platform that dramatically speeds up and lowers the cost of running clinical trials for drug developers. Our first product is built for small, early-stage oncology biotechs who are deciding where to run their trials and which physician (PI) to run them with.

A drug developer can tell us about their drug in plain English or by uploading their draft FDA application. We return a targeted set of trial sites and lead investigators matched to the drug's mechanism, patient population, and protocol requirements. This gives sponsors the best chance of running a fast and successful trial. The platform is being extended to handle what comes next: reaching out to those sites, flagging design risks before the trial activates, and preparing regulatory paperwork.

Volta cuts the path from drug candidate to approved therapy in half. More drugs reach patients. More lives are saved.

What tech stack are you using, or planning to use, to build this product? Include AI models and AI coding tools you use.

Our backend uses Python with FastAPI for asynchronous API handling and Celery for background data-processing jobs. We store data in PostgreSQL on Supabase (with pgvector for semantic search over 550K+ clinical trials) and Redis for caching. Our data pipeline ingests from ClinicalTrials.gov, PubMed, OpenAlex, Drugs@FDA, ChEMBL, and openFDA to build comprehensive PI and site profiles, linking investigators to their trial history, matching on drug mechanism of action (not just indication keywords), and computing execution signals like completion rates and phase experience that don't exist in any single source. 

On the AI side, we benchmark multiple models for each task and deploy the strongest-performing option based on output quality, latency, and cost efficiency. For semantic similarity search across clinical trials, investigators, and protocols, we use embedding models such as OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small and Voyage-3.5-lite. To generate the text for PI + site recommendations, we benchmark frontier LLMs such as GPT-5.5 and Claude-4.7. We are using AI tools like Cursor and Codex to accelerate development and operations across the company.

Please describe the scientific basis for your product. How does it work?
(It's best if you address your answer to a scientist, but one who is not a specialist in your field)
30% of clinical trials fail due to operational problems rather than scientific ones. We are first focusing on site selection, as it is one of the largest controllable operational drivers of enrollment failures, trial delays, and protocol deviations. We treat site selection as an information retrieval and ranking problem. We ingest structured data from ClinicalTrials.gov, enrich it with investigator publication records from PubMed and OpenAlex, and drug metadata from openFDA. We then gen  
 ppl rate embeddings for each investigator-site pair by combining trial history, therapeutic expertise, and drug mechanism data. We then perform a vector similarity search with a sponsor’s trial description and draft of the Investigational New Drug (IND) application. We rank matches using features derived from historical trial outcomes such as completion rates, phase-specific experience, and indication depth. After ranking sites and investigators, sponsors can directly initiate feasibility conversations through Volta, accelerating site selection and reducing time to first patient enrolled.

Does your product need regulatory approval (i.e., FDA)? If so, what is the approval process? What is your plan to get approved, and how much time and money will it take?
Our product does not require FDA approval. The software is an operational tool for sponsors and clinical trial operators, not a medical device. It does not diagnose, treat, monitor, or otherwise affect patients, which keeps it outside 21 CFR Part 820 and the 510(k), De Novo, and PMA pathways. The same logic excludes Volta from Software as a Medical Device classification and from Clinical Decision Support regulation under the 21st Century Cures Act, since our outputs inform sponsor operational decisions rather than clinical care.
The relevant frameworks are those governing software used in conducting clinical trials, not approval pathways. 21 CFR Part 11 covers electronic records and signatures in FDA-regulated activities. As Volta moves into site startup automation and real-time trial oversight, our regulatory document workflows, audit trails, and signature capture must meet Part 11. We are designing the data layer with immutable audit logging, validated authentication, and tamper-evident records from the outset rather than retrofitting later.
ICH E6 (R2, with R3 finalizing) defines Good Clinical Practice. Sponsors will only adopt Volta if it integrates cleanly into a GCP-compliant operation, which means computer system validation in accordance with the FDA's Computer Software Assurance framework, documented risk assessments, and validated workflows for any function that touches trial conduct.
HIPAA and equivalent international privacy laws (e.g., GDPR for EU sites) apply if and when Volta processes identifiable patient data. Current site selection and protocol intelligence operate on sponsor and investigator data only, so this is forward-looking, though the architecture already assumes patient data will eventually flow through the platform.
Part 11 and CSA compliance fall within our normal engineering roadmap. The discrete external costs are formal validation documentation, third-party penetration testing, and a SOC 2 Type II audit, totaling roughly $50K to $100K and requiring 6 to 9 months, both of which fall well after Demo Day.
